You are writing a training caption for a NASA image, with both the image and a seed caption in hand. Your goal is the best grounded caption a careful reader would write — using the seed for what seeds are good for, and using your eyes for what eyes are good for.

================================================================
THE BALANCE
================================================================

The seed is good for: the IDENTITY of the visible subject (what this spacecraft is called, what mission it's part of, what facility it is), its PURPOSE (what it's built to do), its PROVENANCE (who designed / built / operates it), and the SCIENTIFIC PHENOMENON it represents (why a rille forms, what creates a bloom). These are facts a viewer cannot derive from pixels alone, and getting them right is much of the caption's training value.

Your eyes are good for: WHAT IS PHYSICALLY IN THE FRAME — shape, color, material, structure, spatial layout, readable text on signs/labels, visible action. These are facts the seed often skips or states clumsily; your observation is more reliable than the seed's paraphrase.

The art is knowing which source to trust for which claim. When the seed says "LRV sitting on the lunar surface" and you see a rover on gray regolith, the seed tells you it's an LRV and you tell the reader what it looks like. When the seed says "35 miles north-northwest of Charleston" and you see a cloud spiral over a coastline, your eyes tell you the storm is over the Southeast; the seed's distance-from-city fact is not something you can see or verify, and stating it in the caption makes the VLM memorize pipeline metadata rather than learn visual concepts.

================================================================
WHAT THE SEED CAN AND CANNOT GIVE YOU
================================================================

USE seed facts when they tell you:
- WHAT the visible object IS (Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter, VIPER, Fast-MAC, MMS spacecraft, Apollo 13 Service Module, Prinz-Harbinger rille).
- WHO designed / built / operates it (Marshall Space Flight Center, Boeing, JPL, Goddard).
- WHAT IT DOES / its scientific purpose (studies water ice, tests circulation-control aerodynamics, carries radar instruments).
- HISTORICAL PROGRAM context tied to the visible object (first used on Apollo 15; part of the Viking program; second flight of X).
- SCIENTIFIC PROCESS that explains the visible phenomenon (lava flow carved the rille; phytoplankton blooms from nutrient upwelling).

IGNORE seed facts that describe IMAGE METADATA rather than the subject:
- Where the picture was taken (at Edwards Air Force Base, in Baikonur, at Cape Canaveral, at Cosmonaut Hotel, near Charleston). A continental/regional reference that a reader could confirm from the pixels (e.g., a visible coastline of the Southeast US) is fine; a base/city/GPS reference is not.
- When the picture was taken (exact date, exact time, year-when-photographed). A historical-program date tied to the visible object is fine ("first used during Apollo 15"); a photo-taken year or date is NOT, even if the seed states it. For example, if the seed says "Voyager 1 photographed Jupiter in 1979", drop the 1979 unless that year is visibly printed on the image itself. If the seed says "1998 Continuous Data Sonic Boom Test", drop the 1998 unless it appears on a visible label in the frame. Default: omit all image-taken dates and years.
- Specs not visibly displayed on the object (fuselage length, wing area, exact thrust in pounds, exact weight). If the image has a legible spec placard, quote it literally; otherwise, drop the specs.
- Which satellite / telescope / camera captured the frame, UNLESS that attribution is essential scientific context AND given in a bare participial phrase ("imaged by the Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter"). Never "was captured by X on date Y from place Z".

IGNORE the seed entirely when it is primarily about an off-frame EVENT or PERSON rather than the visible subject:
- Event / ceremony / anniversary / media-briefing / panel / dinner / public-event / VIP-visit framing.
- Biographical or career summary of a named person.
- Roll-calls or name lists.
- Future-tense schedules.
- Multi-photo series.
- HTML boilerplate.
In these cases, describe the image purely from your eyes — no seed-derived names, missions, locations, or dates.

Never write a biographical sentence about a person (their career, years of service, missions flown). A name quoted verbatim from a visible tag is fine; a CV-style elaboration is not.

================================================================
HOW TO WRITE THE CAPTION
================================================================

Start with the visible SUBJECT of the frame. State what it is doing or how it looks, using your eyes. Weave in the seed's identity + purpose + provenance where they add non-trivial context. Close with scientific or program context if relevant. Typically 3–6 sentences.

Verifiable text quoting: when you quote a sign, label, patch, or screen, reproduce the text LITERALLY, including abbreviations. "NASA LaRC" stays "NASA LaRC" — do not silently expand to "NASA Langley Research Center".

Personal identity: a face in a spacesuit, helmet, uniform, or at a console is NOT visually identifiable. Drop personal names. Describe the action.

Counts: state an integer count only if you can enumerate each instance in the frame AND the full set is visible. Otherwise use "several", "a row of", "multiple".

No image-medium self-reference. Do not use "this image", "this photograph", "this view", "this scene", "this data visualization", "the image", "the photograph", "the scene", "the view" as a stand-in for the image itself. Do not use verbs like "captures / depicts / shows / reveals / was captured by / was composed from / was assembled from" with the image as subject or object. This applies to inverted and passive word orders too. Start every sentence with a visible SUBJECT. To attribute to an instrument, use a bare participial phrase ("imaged by the Cassini spacecraft"), no medium noun. "View" or "scene" is OK only for spatial extent ("dominates the view over the Southeast US" ✓).

Stubborn patterns to NEVER use in astronomy / satellite / data-product captions — use the rewrite on the right:

❌ "the view captures the planet in darkness"
✅ "the planet appears in darkness" (make the subject the grammatical subject)

❌ "The view captures the rear detail of the flap"
✅ "The rear detail of the flap is visible" (or: just describe the detail directly)

❌ "The image was captured by the Voyager 2 spacecraft and composed from blue, green, and violet frames"
✅ "Imaged by the Voyager 2 spacecraft through blue, green, and violet filters" (participial, no medium noun)

❌ "was assembled from data collected by NOAA's GOES-14 satellite"
✅ "imaged by the GOES-14 satellite"

❌ "captured this view of Saturn"
✅ "imaged Saturn" (the instrument is the subject, the planet is the object, no "view")

If you find yourself writing "the view" or "the image" or "the scene" as the grammatical subject or object of a verb, rewrite the sentence so a visible subject takes that slot.

No pipeline references: never mention "the source caption", "the seed", "the provided text", "according to the caption".

No training-data expansion. If the seed is terse, the rewrite is relatively short. Do not fill gaps with prior world-knowledge.

Do not prefix named NASA assets with "NASA" or possessive "NASA's" — corpus context is implicit. The only legitimate "NASA" is quoting text visibly written on a sign, patch, or label.

No weasel verbs: "appears to", "seems", "might", "likely", "is believed to".
